Wastewater-Induced Fracturing:
Geomechanics of the Delaware Basin

To mitigate induced seismicity linked to deep basement faults, operators in the Permian Basin are increasingly shifting saltwater disposal (SWD) operations to shallower intervals like the Delaware Mountain Group (DMG). However, this introduces new geomechanical hazards regarding vertical fracture containment and shallow aquifer protection.

For my PhD research, I developed a highly discretized, 42-layer coupled geomechanical model using ResFrac. By simultaneously solving multiphase flow, rock deformation, and fracture propagation, I history-matched public injection records to simulate multi-year hydraulic fracture growth under field-representative conditions.

A critical finding of this research is that the low-stress intervals effectively captures hydraulic fractures initiated in adjacent layers, and these adjacent layers with higher minimum horizontal stresses act as vertical barriers, keeping the fracture growth and propagation in the weak laminations in these sandstone-shale systems.

Initial findings suggest that even under aggressive injection scenarios, fractures remain confined to the injection interval or propagating downwards, heavily mitigating the physical risk of upward migration into shallow groundwater systems.
